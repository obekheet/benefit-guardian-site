No annual fee — so the only question is how much it earns you

There's no $95 or $325 fee to claw back here. With a $0 annual fee, the Freedom Unlimited can't cost you money in fees, so the honest question isn't "is it worth it?" but "how much will it actually earn?" That depends entirely on where you spend. Here's the math for three kinds of cardholder.

The everyday flat-rate spender

The card's backbone is 1.5% cash back on everything with no caps or categories to track. If you put $1,500/month of general spend on it, that's $18,000/year at 1.5% = $270/year in cash back for zero effort, on a card that costs nothing to hold. For someone who wants one simple card, that flat 1.5% is the whole pitch — and it's hard to argue with on a no-fee card.

Flat-rate spender: ~$270/year on $1,500/month, $0 fee.

The dining-and-drugstore optimizer

Beyond the flat rate, the card pays 3% on dining (including takeout and eligible delivery), 3% at drugstores, and 5% on travel booked through Chase Travel. Someone who routes $400/month of dining and $100/month of drugstore spend here earns 3% on $6,000/year = $180, plus 1.5% on the rest. The 5% Chase Travel rate is the sleeper: book a $2,000 trip through the portal and that's $100 back. Pair this card with a Sapphire and the points become transferable to airline and hotel partners — a common "Chase trifecta" move.

Category optimizer: $180+ from 3% categories, plus the flat 1.5% on everything else.

The credits-and-perks user

Two small extras sweeten a no-fee card: 6 months of complimentary DashPass (Chase lists an estimated $59.94 value) and a $10 quarterly DoorDash credit on non-restaurant orders ($40/year). If you'd use DoorDash anyway, that's up to ~$100 of first-year value on a card with no fee — pure upside. We don't bank DashPass in a "net" figure since it only matters if you order delivery.

Perk user: up to ~$100 first-year DoorDash value on a $0-fee card, if you'd use it.

The gotchas — what the headline value hides

  • 5% travel is Chase-Travel-only. The 5% rate applies only to travel booked in the Chase Travel portal — book directly with an airline or hotel and you earn the flat 1.5% instead.
  • "Cash back" is really Ultimate Rewards points. You can redeem for cash at 1 cent each, but the points are worth more transferred to travel partners — which requires pairing with a Sapphire or Ink card. On its own, the Freedom Unlimited can't transfer points.
  • The DoorDash credit is non-restaurant only. The $10 quarterly credit applies to grocery/retail DoorDash orders, not restaurant meals, and it doesn't roll over quarter to quarter.
  • DashPass is only 6 months. The complimentary membership is for the first 6 months after activation, then it lapses unless you pay.
  • The 2% Lyft rate is temporary. It's scheduled to run only through September 30, 2027.

What we left out of the tables — and why

  • The sign-up bonus. Chase runs a new-cardmember cash-back bonus (recently a $200-style offer after a minimum spend), but these offers change without notice, so we don't treat it as a verified, dated line or bank it in the math — check Chase's current terms when you apply.
  • A single dollar value for points. Redeemed as cash, points are 1 cent each; transferred to partners via a paired Sapphire/Ink card, they can be worth more. Any single valuation would be a guess.
  • Foreign transaction behavior. The Freedom Unlimited charges a foreign transaction fee, so we don't list "no FX fees" as a perk — worth knowing before you travel abroad with it.
