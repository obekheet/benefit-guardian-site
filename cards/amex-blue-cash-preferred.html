Is it actually worth $95? Find yourself below.

The first year is free; after that the fee is $95. This card's value is built on two things you can actually count: 6% back at U.S. supermarkets (on up to $6,000 of spend per year) and a $120 Disney Bundle credit — plus 6% on select streaming, 3% on gas and transit. Whether it pays off comes down almost entirely to how much you spend on groceries. Here's the honest math for three kinds of cardholder.

The big-grocery-budget cardholder

If you spend the full $6,000/year at U.S. supermarkets, 6% returns $360 in cash back from groceries alone. Add the $120 Disney Bundle credit (if you'd pay for Disney+, Hulu or ESPN+ anyway), and you're at $480 in countable value before gas, transit, or streaming — against a $95 fee. That's a net of roughly +$385/year, plus whatever 3% gas and transit add on top.

Maxes the $6,000 grocery cap + uses the Disney credit: about +$385/year before other categories.

The average-family cardholder

A more typical household spending ~$500/month ($6,000/year) on groceries still maxes the cap, but many families spend less — say $300/month, or $3,600/year, which at 6% is $216. If you also use the $120 Disney credit, that's $336 in value against the $95 fee — a comfortable +$241/year, before adding 3% gas and transit. For most grocery-spending families, this card clears its fee with room to spare.

~$300/month groceries + Disney credit: roughly +$241/year, before gas and transit.

The light-grocery cardholder

If you rarely shop at supermarkets — you eat out, or buy groceries at warehouse clubs and Walmart, which Amex does not count as "U.S. supermarkets" — the 6% rate may apply to very little of your spending. Without the grocery volume and without using the Disney credit, the $95 fee can easily outrun what you earn back.

If that's you, a no-annual-fee cash-back card (including Amex's own Blue Cash Everyday) may return more for your pattern. The Preferred only wins if you feed it real supermarket spending.

Low supermarket spend, no Disney credit: the $95 fee can exceed the rewards.

The gotchas — what the headline value hides

  • The 6% groceries rate is capped at $6,000/year. After $6,000 in supermarket spend, the rate drops to 1%. The most the 6% category can return is $360 a year.
  • "U.S. supermarkets" is narrower than it sounds. Amex excludes warehouse clubs (Costco, Sam's Club), superstores and big-box stores (Walmart, Target), and some specialty grocers. If you buy groceries there, they likely earn just 1%.
  • There's a $95 annual fee after year one. The first year is free, but from year two on you need enough grocery spend (and/or the Disney credit) to clear $95 before the card pays off.
  • The Disney credit requires an enrolled, recurring subscription. The $10 monthly credit only posts when you pay for an eligible Disney Bundle subscription with the card — it's not free money if you wouldn't otherwise subscribe.
  • The 6% streaming list is specific. Only select U.S. streaming services qualify; check Amex's current list before assuming your service counts.

What we left out of the table — and why

  • The welcome offer. Blue Cash Preferred typically advertises up to $300 cash back after meeting a minimum spend (recently ~$3,000 in the first months). Because welcome offers change without notice, we don't bank it in the steady-state math — check Amex's current terms when you'd apply.
  • The exact dollar value of 3% gas, transit, and streaming. These are real rates, but their value depends entirely on your spending in each category, which we can't stamp from a benefits page. We fold them in as "additional return" rather than inventing a figure.
  • Reward Dollar redemption nuances. Cash back posts as Reward Dollars redeemable for statement credit; we treat $1 of Reward Dollars as $1, which is how Amex redeems them for statement credit.
