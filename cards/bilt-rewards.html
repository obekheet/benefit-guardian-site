The Bilt Card, every tier — verified and dated

Bilt's 2.0 lineup comes in three flavors — Blue, Obsidian and Palladium. We pulled every benefit straight from Bilt's own published pages, dated it, and added honest math so you can see which tier (if any) is worth it for how you actually spend.

Is it worth it?

Bilt's pitch is simple: it's one of the few cards that lets you earn points on rent without a transaction fee. Whether any tier pays off depends on how you spend. Here's the honest math for three common situations.

The renter

You pay $1,800/mo rent and want points for it. Bilt Blue ($0 fee) earns Bilt Points on rent plus 1X on everyday purchases, with no annual fee to claw back. If you transfer those points to airline or hotel partners, the value can beat a flat cash-back card — but only if you actually redeem through travel.

Best free entry point if you pay rent.

The diner & traveler

Bilt Obsidian adds 3X on dining or groceries, 2X on travel, a $100 annual Bilt Travel hotel credit and trip-delay coverage. If you spend heavily on food and travel, the higher multipliers and the travel credit can outweigh its fee — do the math on your real spend before upgrading.

Worth it for big dining/travel spenders.

The perks seeker

Bilt Palladium piles on a $400 annual Bilt Travel hotel credit, $200 annual Bilt Cash, Priority Pass lounge access, Bilt Gold status and purchase protection. Add up the credits you'll genuinely use; if they clear the (higher) fee, the perks are real. If you won't use the lounges or travel credit, skip it.

Only if you'll use the premium credits.

We don't earn a commission on any tier, so this isn't a nudge to upgrade — it's just the numbers. Annual fees vary by tier; confirm the current fee on Bilt's page before you apply.

The gotchas

The honest caveats most pages skip. None of these are dealbreakers on their own — just know them before you apply.

  • •Points are most valuable through travel transfers. Bilt Points shine when you transfer them 1:1 to airline and hotel partners. Redeem them for things like Amazon or fitness and the per-point value usually drops. If you won't book travel, a flat cash-back card may serve you better.
  • •Rent earning has rules. Bilt's whole appeal is fee-free rent points, but earning typically depends on meeting a monthly transaction requirement and on how you pay. Check Bilt's current terms so you don't miss out.
  • •Annual fees vary by tier. Blue is $0, but Obsidian and Palladium carry fees. The premium credits only pay off if you actually use them — add up the ones you'll really redeem before upgrading.
  • •This is the new "2.0" lineup. Bilt relaunched its cards, so older guides you find elsewhere may describe the previous single-card product. We sourced this page to Bilt's current published pages, dated June 1, 2026.

What we left out — and why

We only list benefits we could verify against Bilt's own published pages. A few things you might expect to see, we deliberately didn't include:

  • •Exact annual fees per tier. Fees can change and weren't part of our verified source set, so we point you to Bilt's page rather than print a number that might be stale.
  • •Point valuations. "Bilt Points are worth X cents" figures are estimates that depend on how you redeem. We list what each tier earns and let you decide.
  • •Which specific airline and hotel partners. We confirmed 17 airline and 6 hotel transfer partners, but partner lists shift — check Bilt's transfer page for the live roster before counting on a specific one.
